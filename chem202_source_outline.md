## Experiment 1: Thermochemistry and Neutralization Reactions

- Thermochemistry studies heat/thermal energy changes that accompany chemical reactions.
- Reaction types listed: formation, decomposition/separation, single replacement, double replacement, combustion, neutralization.
- Neutralization reaction: reaction between an acid and a base that produces salt and water, meaning an aqueous salt solution.
- Examples:
  - `NaOH + HCl -> NaCl + H2O`
  - `KOH + HNO3 -> KNO3 + H2O`
- Base: substance able to give hydroxide ion `OH-`.
- Acid: substance able to give hydrogen ion `H+`.
- Salt: material made from a metallic part and a nonmetallic part.
- Aqueous solution: result of acid-base reaction; salt dissolved in solution.
- In the experiment, equal volumes can be mixed, such as `20 mL` acid with `20 mL` base.
- Heat equation: `q = Cp * m * Delta T`.
- `Cp` for the solution is approximately `4.18 J/g.C`.
- Total solution mass: `m = V * d`.
- Temperature change: `Delta T = T2 - T1`.
- Relationship between heat and enthalpy change: `Delta H = -q`.
- If q is negative in the reaction, the reaction is exothermic; if q is positive, the reaction is endothermic.

## Experiment 2: Solutions and Their Concentrations

- Solution types listed: true solutions, colloidal solutions, suspended solutions.
- Concentration: expression of amount of solute in a defined volume per liter of solution or solvent.
- Molality `m`: amount of solute in moles in one kilogram of solvent.
- Molality equation: `m = n / mass of solvent (kg)`.
- Percentage concentration is expressed using `%`.
- Very small concentrations can be expressed as parts per million `ppm` or parts per billion `ppb`.

## Experiment 3: How to Calculate Molarity

- Molarity `M`: number of moles of solute in one liter of solution.
- Molarity equation: `M = n / V_L`.
- `M` means molarity.
- `n` means number of moles.
- Number of moles equation: `n = Mass / M.wt`.
- `V_L` means solution volume in liters.

## Experiment 4: Solubility

- Solubility: amount of solute in a defined volume of solvent.
- Dissolution temperature: temperature required to completely dissolve the solute, such as salt, in the solution.
- Factors affecting solubility: temperature, amount of solute, amount of solvent, type of stirring used, type of solute.
- Relationship with heat: relationship between solute mass and heat is direct. Increasing solute mass requires a higher temperature to dissolve it.
- Solubility unit listed: `g/mL`.
- Salt order by heat need from lowest to highest:
  1. Organic salts.
  2. Inorganic complex salts.
  3. Inorganic simple salts.

## Experiment 5: Electrical Conductivity

- Electrolyte solution: solution capable of electrical conduction because it contains freely moving ions.
- Meaning of `5% NaCl` solution:
  - For every `100 mL` of total solution, there are `5 g` of `NaCl`.
  - Another way: add `5 g` salt to `95 mL` water/solvent.
- In this experiment context, the relationship between temperature and electrical conductivity is inverse.
- Reason listed: higher temperature increases solvent evaporation, which affects ion movement and reduces electrical conductivity.

## Experiment 6: Calculating the Freezing Point Depression Constant

- The experiment calculates the molal freezing-point constant of benzene `Kf`.
- Formula: `Kf = (W * 128 * Delta T) / 1000`.
- `W`: mass of benzene. The file notes taking `10 mL` benzene and then measuring its mass.
- `Delta T`: difference in temperature degrees, `T2 - T1`.
- `T1`: freezing point of pure benzene.
- `T2`: freezing point of the solution, benzene plus naphthalene.
- Chemical formulas to memorize:
  - Benzene: `C6H6`.
  - Naphthalene: `C10H8`.
  - Acetone: `CH3COCH3`.
